'Hillary and Clinton' review: When Bill tried to save Hillary

Chris JonesContact Reporter

Somewhere, deep in your political memory — now trumped by another contest, I know — you may recall the Democratic primaries of 2008 when a woman named Hillary fought a young man named Barack. Although the former first lady was the better debate performer (at least true at the debate I attended), much chatter ensued about the incontrovertible competence but coolness of that year's Clinton candidate, in rich contrast to her demonstrably sinful but loquacious and charming spouse.

Hillary Clinton was, you might remember, under pressure back then to bring in Bill, the better to feel the electorate's pain, although such an act would clearly come at a price with a candidate who also was charged with some need to disprove spousal privilege and stand alone. The dark night of Hillary's soul that accompanied that dilemma forms the basis of "Hillary and Clinton," the new play by Lucas Hnath, now in its world-premiere production at the Victory Gardens Theater.

Let me put some cards on the table. I think "Hillary and Clinton" is an audacious, whip-smart, highly entertaining piece of writing, all the more valuable for its appearance in the middle of another primary battle, albeit in an America changed for the good, at least as far as what Hillary Clinton has to answer for is concerned. You no longer hear talk of any need to channel her husband's charm, or to be more "feeling" or whatever. But she's still there, of course, and still not yet a nominee, and thus "Hillary and Clinton" also has a political immediacy generally lacking in the theater, where plays get locked and set much too far in advance, and where some deep diving into the ideology or emotion of the moment thus becomes all too rare.

I also think that "Hillary and Clinton" will enjoy better subsequent productions than the premiere staging, which, although it has a few great moments, does not feel to me to be especially well cast.

The setup is a hotel room. Hillary (Cheryl Lynn Bruce) is hanging around, losing. Her campaign manager (played by Keith Kupferer) does not want to see Bill Clinton in the state. Her opponent might be willing to offer her the vice president's spot on the ticket, if she backs off campaigning. Of course, Bill (John Apicella) comes anyway, complete with much advice, and so does the Other Guy (Juan Francisco Villa) with his tempting offer of expedient surrender.

In the first few moments of the play, the audience is asked to view these real-world characters at a certain remove, as if in a parallel universe. This is Hnath's — unnecessary — hedge against literalism and impersonation. We do not need actors who look like the Clintons, nor the elliptically named Other Guy. The play is more complex, and thus better, if they do not.

Alas, the director Chay Yew has, in Bruce, an actress whose emotional underpinning is not only as palpable as her warm humanity but far from the Hillary Clinton personality, at least as it was in 2008 (I think it's different now). Frankly, Bruce feels more of a Bill. Meanwhile, Apicella plays Bill as so low status, tense and, well, schlumplike, you never get the sense that this, allegedly, was the social charmer, if not the brains, of the pair and you certainly don't feel he could play the role of savior.

These are both very capable actors, but something feels off. Let's stipulate again that they are not playing the real figures, but if they don't convey the essence as we perceive it, then I'd argue it hurts the issues of the play. Villa comes the closest to working, although even he backs off a little when the real Other Guy in 2008 would have pressed his case. He doesn't give Bruce's Hillary enough to fight against.

The casting issues are compounded by a lack of internal pace — this is a very short play, but it would have been even better if played more quickly, especially given the quartet of formidable intellects on the stage.

All that said, I still think you should go — "Hillary and Clinton" is an important piece of writing, and Yew offers an uncluttered approach to the work on William Boles' lean, bare-walls setting.

The show has a Mametian verbal excitement, without all the current baggage. It homes in on our inability to respond ideologically unless someone feeds us an appealing personal narrative (such as the one Trump currently is selling). In one of the most potent exchanges, an exasperated Hillary tells her shrewd, storyteller husband, the one who keeps accusing her of having no personal yarn akin to "The Man From Hope," that "any new story I give them is just a less-interesting second act to the first."

Indeed. It took another eight years for her (and us) to get past that.

And most important of all, "Hillary and Clinton" probes the potential power of exposed weakness, another oft-overlooked Trump skill from the master of the pivot. Perfection in the political landscape is intimidating and off-putting, although many candidates past and present have failed to appreciate that truth. Flaws can get you elected — especially if you have a certain facility with the redeemed-sinner narrative, as, of course, did one of the Clintons.

Maybe both. Who would know?

But this most certainly is a play to make you wonder.
