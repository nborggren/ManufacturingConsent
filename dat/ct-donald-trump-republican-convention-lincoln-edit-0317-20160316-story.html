Let the convention decide: Party of Lincoln or party of Trump

Editorial Board

In May 1860, the nascent Republican Party found its footing in Chicago, in a hastily built pine-plank convention center known as the Wigwam. There, on the corner of what is now Wacker Drive and Lake Street, the party of abolition became the party of Lincoln as the thoughtful traveling attorney from Springfield took the presidential nomination over more prominent contenders.

The Chicago Tribune was on hand for Abraham Lincoln's dramatic selection — oh, were we there: to record the event, but also to cheerlead. Our co-owner and managing editor at the time, Joseph Medill, had helped found the Republican Party. He recognized sooner than most that Lincoln was the best choice to become president, to end slavery, to lead the nation to prosperity. He pushed every button to get his guy.

Medill's Tribune endorsed Lincoln in February 1860, describing him as "a man of, at once, great breadth and great acuteness of intellect … never garrulous, never promising what he cannot perform, never doing anything for show or effect, laboriously attentive to detail, industrious and conscientious."

Notice how that description contrasts with the coarse belligerence of this year's Republican front-runner. It's not entirely fair to compare any candidate to Lincoln. But still. As the page that looks to President Lincoln as its lodestar and believes in Midwestern values, we are alarmed at how far politics has fallen.

In our endorsement last week, the Tribune Editorial Board dismissed Donald Trump and his reckless rhetoric, citing in part Mitt Romney's impassioned takedown of the candidate who cheers assaults on protesters and applauds the prospect of twisting the Constitution. We endorsed Marco Rubio as best representing the GOP message of opportunity and uplift.

On Tuesday, we watched with disheartenment as Trump rolled on toward the nomination. He sent Rubio packing with a victory in Florida, and collected Illinois too. Ohio Gov. John Kasich won his home state, setting up a three-way contest with Sen. Ted Cruz that could lead to the showdown we now hope to see: a contested convention in Cleveland.

True, we're still in the thick of primary season, when it's more effective for candidates to go hard-core than to explore nuance. But Trump, who has never held elected office, shows little aptitude for statesmanship or conscientious leadership. Certainly we don't recognize his fidelity to Republican principles. What is Donald Trump's political lodestar? We struggle to answer.

He's a self-described free-trader who trash-talks trade deals and says he'll force American companies to invest in the United States. He promises to build an enormous wall on our southern border and get Mexico to pay for it.

We hear from him no coherent world view, nor can he point to any advisers on international affairs who might articulate his perspective. He's advocated for closing the country to Muslims and killing the family members of terrorist suspects. There is an impetuousness to him that is inappropriate for a presidential candidate, let alone a president.

The optimistic interpretation of Trump's behavior is that he'll modulate over time, once he's won the job. Until then, he's running a visceral campaign as an outsider with an uncanny ability to tap into the nation's worries. He is the rare protest candidate who speaks effectively to the population at large. "We're going to make America great again," Trump promises. Who doesn't want that?

A sidenote: We invited Trump to visit us at the Tribune last June to talk about his building across the street. You know, the one with the giant illuminated "Trump" sign. By the time he arrived he already had descended an escalator at Trump Tower in New York and announced his candidacy. We enjoyed the conversation and showmanship. We certainly didn't think we were meeting the next president of the United States.

Since then, it's been a continual ride down the decorum escalator for Trump. There is a nasty edge to his pronouncements that translates into disrespect and intolerance, which can trigger an air of violence at his rallies. Perhaps the low point was Las Vegas in February, when Trump responded to a protester by saying "I'd like to punch him in the face." Or maybe it was Wednesday morning on CNN, when Trump imagined the trouble facing Republicans at the convention if he loses the nomination: "I think you'd have riots," Trump said. "I'm representing ... many, many millions of people, in many cases first-time voters."

We stand at a crossroads in this nomination cycle, for the Republican Party and those who believe in the values of Lincoln. The nomination is still up for grabs. Kasich's victory appears to increase the odds that no candidate will acquire the 1,237-delegate majority to secure victory before the convention this summer in Cleveland. We hope voters in remaining contests will thwart a Trump win by supporting Cruz or Kasich. We urge Republicans to then unite around the goal of choosing a candidate other than Trump at the convention.

We aren't advocating an act of political sedition. The nominating process is designed to pick a candidate either through the primaries and caucuses or, if no outright winner emerges, at the convention. While many voters are choosing Trump, the party is deeply divided. Trump has not won a majority in any state and was at or below 35 percent in 12 states.

The rules for a contested convention give the edge to the front-runner on the first ballot, but if no one breaks the 1,237 mark, it gets interesting. Nearly 75 percent of the delegates are released from any voting obligation for the second round of balloting, and even more delegates become free agents in succeeding rounds. Trump getting bumped? It could happen.

Once more we'll look to Lincoln for inspiration. Back in 1860, it was a boisterous, over-the-top convention in Chicago, with Lincoln as the dark horse facing William Seward and Salmon P. Chase. Our man Medill was there, using some unscrupulous electioneering to help (no, we don't advocate this).

The balloting passed the first and second rounds without a winner. In the third, Medill rallied enough support to push Lincoln over the top. A cannon boomed to tell Chicago the news.

Let it happen again in Cleveland. Let the cannon boom to signal the end of a raucous convention that selects a candidate worthy of the Republican nomination. Worthy of our country.
