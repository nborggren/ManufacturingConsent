In 2013, Ethan Couch, a well-off Texas teenager, killed four people. With a potent cocktail of marijuana and Valium in his system, he was driving on a restricted license. His attorneys argued that he suffered from "affluenza" — a term "that dates at least to the 1980s to describe the psychological problems that can afflict children of privilege," as The New York Times put it.

Having violated his stunning sentence of just 10 years probation late last year, Ethan and his mother sparked an international manhunt by slipping off to Puerta Vallarta, Mexico. Mother and son were soon captured and are in Texas custody.

This week, Emory University — the Harvard of the South that costs $65,000 a year to attend — became the latest American university to demonstrate that affluenza might come in different forms. I'll let the school's newspaper, The Emory Wheel, speak for itself with this Tuesday report: "Students protested yesterday at the Emory Administration Building following a series of overnight, apparent pro-Donald Trump for president chalkings throughout campus."

In case you're wondering, "chalkings" is not a code for some sort of nightmarish Donald Trump cult induction or a euphemism for those scary outlines that police draw around dead bodies.

No, the "chalkings" causing fear, loathing and consternation at Emory were as simple as they sound: the writing of words, done in chalk. Those words said, in most cases — get your safe space ready — "Trump 2016."

At the sight of these eye-burning lines constructed out of a writing tool frequently deployed by 5-year-olds on sidewalks, around 40 to 50 students marched into an administrative building, the Emory Wheel reports. Their leader, a pensive and lugubrious sophomore, sounded the alarm: "It is our duty to fight for our freedom. It is our duty to win. We must love each other and support each other. We have nothing to lose but our chains."

I really hate to quote Trump, because he's one bad proverbial burrito wrapped in questionable golden foil, but sometimes he says it best: "Sad!" If you're a normal person and you've spent your life working hard and earning a living and actually facing a real problem or crisis or two, you're probably thinking the same thing about this whole Emory brouhaha.

Look, Emory students, we get it: The rise of Trump might be traumatizing. However, whining is not a strategy. Also, if somebody decides to write "Trump 2016" all over your campus in a transitory medium that disappears with the first drops of the next rain, you might not want to indulge in an over-the-top, hysterical freakout that the chalk-writers were likely trying to inspire. Alas, this is exactly what happened at Emory.

Emory's president, James W. Wagner, was "called into the board room by students and listened at the head of the table while they described how the appearance of the chalkings made them feel," the Emory Wheel reports. (In case you're wondering, those feelings included "frustration," "fear" and trauma. Copious tears also were shed.)

"What do we have to do for you to listen to us?" the students asked Wagner, to which he responded, to his credit, "Good gracious! How on earth are you going to survive in the real world, you goofy participation-trophy loons?"

Just kidding!

Here is how Wagner actually responded: "What actions should I take?" He later followed up with a sympathetic campuswide email, paired with a promise to review footage from security cameras in an attempt to find and punish the pro-Trump chalkers. Emory's student government, meanwhile, has offered emergency counseling for traumatized students.

Meanwhile, an ocean away, Brussels faced the aftermath of savage jihadi suicide bombings that killed at least 30 people and wounded more than 200 others — a horrifying reminder that safe spaces don't really exist. Don't tell that to the affluenza-stricken students at places like Emory, however. Don't try to make them feel better, either. It's the weirdest thing: They seem to be reveling in their "pain."
