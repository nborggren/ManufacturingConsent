Former Secretary of State Hillary Clinton has delivered a sharp blow to Sen. Bernie Sanders' call for a political revolution, but his Connecticut supporters aren't ready to count their man out yet, even as she marches toward the Democratic presidential nomination with a growing lead in delegates.

"I think in a traditional campaign when [the candidate] doesn't perform well the outcome is usually the funding dries up," Carlos Camacho, co-founder of the Bernie Sanders Connecticut Team, said this week after Clinton swept Sanders in Florida, Illinois, North Carolina, Ohio and Missouri. "But with Bernie, people get fired up even more so and flood his donation pages. I think he's got plenty of money to continue the campaign."

Connecticut's political establishment remains staunchly behind Clinton, with Gov. Dannel P. Malloy and members of the state's delegation in Washington, D.C., campaigning on her behalf. Clinton will be in Litchfield County for a fundraiser Friday at an undisclosed location with U.S. Sens. Richard Blumenthal and Chris Murphy, U.S. Rep. Elizabeth Esty, D-5th District, Malloy and Lt. Gov. Nancy Wyman.

Ronald Schurin, a political science professor at UConn, said he thinks Sanders will take his fight all the way to the Democratic National Convention in Philadelphia in July, but after Tuesday's primaries, "barring some totally unimaginable set of circumstances, Hillary has it wrapped up."

Since most states in the Democratic presidential primary award delegates proportionally, Clinton's overwhelming victories in the South will likely trump any wins Sanders may be able to pull off in the coming states where he has been polling better, like Utah and Idaho, which hold Democratic caucuses on Tuesday.

And superdelegates, Democratic elected officials and party leaders who are free to support any candidate regardless of how their state votes, have overwhelmingly backed Clinton.

According to a tally by The Associated Press, Clinton leads Sanders in total delegates, including superdelegates, 1,606 to 851. A total of 2,383 delegates are required to win the nomination. There are 70 delegates at stake in the Connecticut on April 26, a date when there are also primaries in Delaware, Maryland, Pennsylvania and Rhode Island.

However, Sanders supporters point to polls that consistently show the Vermont senator performing better than Clinton in a general election matchup against Donald Trump, the increasingly likely GOP nominee for president.

Al Simon, a Windsor town councilman and Sanders supporter, said he's "in it for 50 states." Simon and others will soon begin gearing up for Connecticut's primary. Camacho said his grass-roots group has been meeting with Sanders campaign staffers and there are plans to open an office in the state. There are also coming rallies and phone banking events.

"It's not finished yet," Simon said. "You don't call the game when it's only half over."

Simon said the situation is reminiscent of 2006, when former U.S. Sen. Joe Lieberman had the backing of the state party but Greenwich cable tycoon Ned Lamont won in the Democratic primary. Lieberman ran on his own ballot line and was re-elected in a three-way race.

"Regular voters are really open to a message of change and in the next four weeks we're going to do everything we can to make the pitch … that Bernie's the leader we need," Simon said.

Shifting back to the electoral map, Schurin said Clinton's largest victories – in states like Alabama, Georgia and Texas – came in areas that reliably vote for Republicans in presidential elections. In swing states, like Ohio and Missouri, the race has been much tighter. And Sanders scored an upset win in Michigan.

A Wall Street Journal/NBC News poll earlier this month said a third of Sanders supporters could not see themselves voting for Clinton in November.

"There's a very large constituency that Hillary needs to win over not just in terms of their votes but in terms of their enthusiasm if she's going to win in November," Schurin said.

Part of that constituency that Clinton needs to make inroads into is younger voters. Millennials continue to overwhelmingly back Sanders. In Ohio, 83 percent of voters younger than 30 supported Sanders, according to exit polls. But Clinton has been buoyed by older voters, who make up a larger chunk of the electorate.

"We still have a path to win … we have to motivate some people who have not been as involved in the political system as they need to be," Simon said.

Rep. Edwin Vargas, D-Hartford, one of a handful of Connecticut lawmakers who has publicly backed Sanders, said Clinton, and the Democratic Party, can't discount the youth movement that has coalesced around the senator from Vermont.

"That's the vote of the future," he said. "That's the electorate of the near future."

When it comes time for Connecticut Democrats to vote, Vargas said, he thinks Sanders will fare well. He pointed to 1992, when California Gov. Jerry Brown narrowly beat Bill Clinton in the state. Regardless of whether he wins or loses the nomination, Sanders has assembled a strong coalition, has pushed Clinton to the left on certain issues and will "leave his mark on the party platform," Vargas said.

In a message to supporters Wednesday, Sanders was upbeat.

"When we started our campaign 10 months ago, I don't think you could find a single person who would believe you if you said Bernie Sanders would win nine states by this point in the campaign. … The whole country will be watching to see how we respond in this moment."
