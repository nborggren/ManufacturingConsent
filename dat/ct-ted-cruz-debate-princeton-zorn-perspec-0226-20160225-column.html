'Anyone but Ted Cruz' — will debaters' tale repeat itself?

If Sen. Ted Cruz of Texas ends up being the beneficiary of the "anyone but Donald Trump" vote in the Republican Party, it will be, for him, a sweet reversal of fortune.

In 1991, when the current GOP presidential hopeful was a junior at Princeton University, he was the victim of an "anyone but Ted Cruz" vote in the American Parliamentary Debate Association. It's a tale that has appeared in several lengthy looks at Cruz's early forays into the persuasive arts.

Parliamentary debate contests are ideal venues for fiery, theatrical orators. They're not the research-intensive, rat-a-tat fact fights of policy debate in which many schools engage, but largely extemporaneous battles of wits in which competitors joust on topics given to them just moments in advance.

The college parliamentary debate circuit of 25 years ago in the Northeast was dominated by "hyper-verbal, hyper-competitive nerds," former Columbia University debater Sacha Zimmerman wrote in a National Journal retrospective last year. And Cruz, who was three years ahead of her, was the Michael Jordan of the league — a "legendary" and "bewitching" speaker whose "extraordinary intelligence was very much his calling card on and off the debate stage," she wrote.

But many fellow competitors found his zeal — a trademark to this day — more than a little off-putting. Zimmerman quoted former Johns Hopkins University debater Ted Niblock saying Cruz was a "scary, driven machine who fought a protracted, bloody land war for total victory" and won matches "in the most grinding, methodical, joyless way possible."

When it became clear in the middle of the 1990-91 school year that the ambitious Cruz intended to run for president of the association for the following year, a group of other students persuaded the comparatively countercultural Niblock to stand against him.

When the members gathered on the Yale University campus as the year came to a close, Cruz spoke first. He was typically forceful and well-spoken in advancing his candidacy, according to the memories of several former association members with whom I checked to confirm the story. He promised to initiate an educational outreach component during his term in office and noted that it was important for the international reputation of the association to choose a skilled debater as its president.

Niblock, who did not want to go on the record for this column, then rose and, in a few nonchalant sentences, said he would do nothing if elected and that the office barely mattered anyway.

The implicit message, according to former top White House economics adviser Austan Goolsbee, who was then a star debater for Yale and in the auditorium at the time, was "my qualification is that I'm not Ted Cruz."

Niblock received a standing ovation, Goolsbee told me.

That Niblock came in first in the balloting, forcing Cruz and another candidate to settle for a co-vice presidency, speaks to the enduring quality of Cruz's now legendary charmlessness. I was assured several times in reporting this story that it wasn't his politics that his peers didn't like — his conservatism was not unusual in debate circles — it was his personality.

And though Cruz wears his unpopularity in Washington as a badge of honor, it speaks to the difficulty he's likely to face in building alliances here and around the world should he be elected. He's not a guy you want to have a beer with. He's a guy you want to see punked.

But that Cruz was in a position to win — that he was so relentless and accomplished as a debater that he was the obvious candidate even months in advance — speaks to the formidable and flexible quality of his intellect.

Those are qualities that are an asset not only for a president but also any candidate for president.

"We debaters understand how he thinks, how he sees a multitude of situations and consequences unfolding in his mind's eye," Zimmerman wrote in the final paragraph of her article. Her headline: "I watched Ted Cruz debate in college. Don't count him out."

I won't. Though I'll pledge my vote right now to any candidate who's not him.
