What Ted Cruz gets wrong about Obama's visit to Cuba

President Barack Obama is visiting Cuba amid a wave of excitement and expectations from the Cuban people.

Steve ChapmanContact Reporter

President Barack Obama’s visit to Cuba represents a historic step, comparable in a scaled-down way to Richard Nixon’s 1972 trip to China. It’s an opening to a longtime adversary based on the hope of bringing changes of benefit to both Americans and the people of the adversary. And it’s drawing conservative reactions similar to those that Nixon’s journey provoked.

Ted Cruz, the son of a Cuban refugee, claims that “Obama has chosen to legitimize the corrupt and oppressive Castro regime with his presence on the island,” sending political prisoners the cruel message that “you’re alone with your tormentors.” Writing in Politico, he insists that freedom “cannot happen by enriching and empowering the dictatorship.”

What Cruz omits is that more than half a century of ostracism and embargo have not achieved the freedom he wants for Cuba. Obama doesn’t expect results instantly or even soon, but he is making a rational calculation that over time, greater human and commercial interaction will stimulate the sort of changes we have been seeking since Fidel Castro gained power.

Where did he get that idea? Maybe from the U.S. experience with China, which has virtually abandoned its old communist economy in favor of a largely capitalist one — something inconceivable in 1972. People in China do not enjoy the right to criticize the government or organize against it, but they are far freer than they were then. And China’s government has become much more responsible and flexible in world affairs.

Obama is playing a long game that may not pay off till long after he has left the White House. And it may not pay off at all — the United States does not always get its way. But we’ve tried the Cruz approach, only to see Cuba remain a repressive dictatorship. Let’s give the president’s strategy a chance and see if it yields better results. I’m guessing we won’t have to wait 50 years to find out.
