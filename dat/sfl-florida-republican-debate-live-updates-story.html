Recap: Trump, Rubio, Cruz, Kasich in 'civil,' issues-oriented debate | Video, photos

Highlights from the University of Miami Republican debate

The 2016 campaign circus descended on the BankUnited Center at the University of Miami Thursday, where four Republican candidates fought through a lengthy debate discussing immigration, education, Social Security, Isis, climate change and more.

Rubio made it to his closing statement before mentioning his bartender father. "Now the moment has arrived for our generation to do our part,"he said as he asked Florida voters to support him. 

Cruz poked at Trump in his closing statement, while listing the candidates' fathers occupations. Then he narrowed in on Hillary Clinton and the November election. 

Trump's closing statement was measured and serious, in direct contrast to his previous debates. He was dire, warning Republicans to embrace people who now love them.  

In his closing statement, Kasich promised to continue to run positive campaign. "I have run an unwavering positive campaign for president of the United States … so my kids can look at the way you can run for president.  I will continue to run a positive campaign.”

 Path to nomination

The GOP candidates addressed the possibility of a brokered convention and whether Rubio, Kasich and Cruz can mathematically reach the nomination. 

Trump  “I’m going to have the delegates.”

Cruz:  “If we nominate Donald Trump, Hillary wins.”

Trump and Cruz seemed to support the idea of choosing a candidate throuugh the primaries, not at a brokered convention. 

Speaking of which, Rubio's campaign had some good news Thursday night. He won one delegate in Hawaii, two days after the primaries there and in Michigan, Mississippi and Idaho where Rubio was shut out. 

There were 150 delegates at stake in Tuesday's Republican contests.

The AP delegate count:

Trump: 459.

Cruz: 360.

Rubio: 152.

Kasich: 54.

Needed to win the nomination: 1,237.

Violence at rallies

Trump, when asked about violence around the country and at his rallies, “When they see what’s going on in this country, they have anger. I certainly do not condone that at all.”

On his seeming to encourage rough treatment of protesters at rallies: “We have some protesters who are some bad dudes, they have done bad things.”

He said it's usually the police who are removing people. Then he shifted to lauding police.

Cruz attacked Trump over asking people to raising hand to swear support to him.  On "Today" show, “they tried to equate it to Nazi Germany.  It was a disgrace [what "Today" show did].”

Kasich said it's more important to unite the country than to promote violence at political rallies.

Kasich didn't mention Trump specifically, but said in Thursday's debate that it's important to tell Americans that their problems can be solved.

Kasich said candidates can “prey” on people's fears, or offer them solutions. He preferred to do the latter.

Climate change

Rubio was asked whether he will acknowledge the 'scientific consensus' on climate change and whether they pledge to do something about it. The question was posed by Miami Mayor Tomas Regalado, who has endorsed Rubio. 

“One of the reasons the climate is changing is because the climate has always been changing,” Rubio siad. 

Rubio said South Florida is mostly built on swampy land. "These laws some people are aksing us to pass" won't help the environment and will bankrupt the U.S. Sea levels would still rise, because "America is a country, not a planet." 

Kasich touts his reduction of emissions in Ohio and says you don't have to choose between jobs and the environment. "We don't know how much humans contribute, but it's important to develop renewable energy."

China

Trump seemed befuddled by question about what he said about China putting down Tiananmen Square protests of 1989. He previously said the Chinese government's actions were strong leaderhship. Pressed to clarify, Trump said, “Strong doesn’t mean good. “

Kasich, who was highly critical of the Chinese government at the time, said, “We should build a statue of that young man over here,” referring to the man who stood in front of a tank.  

Cuba

Dana Bash changes conversation to Cuba, saying Trump agrees with the president -- and most of America -- that the U.S. relationship with Cuba needs to change. 

Rubio, whose parents emigrated from Cuba, responds that he thinks so too, but working with the Castro regime won't make the island more democratic. 

Trump responds that he wants a much better deal with Cuba, urging a cautious measure to reparations and avoiding litigation. He said he would close the embassy in Cuba until the deal changes. 

"I don't know that Cuba is going to sue us, but if they sue in a court in Miami, they are going to lose," Rubio said, eliciting laughs and a round of applause from the audience. 

Cruz, whose father is Cuban, said Trump doesn't understand the issues. “His answer to everything is that everything would be better if only someone smarter were in government.”

Kasich says he won't encourage U.S. companies to do business in Cuba. 

Support for Israel 

Trump defended his approach to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, saying he can be somewhat neutral in order to broker a deal.

While “there's nobody that's more pro-Israel than I am,” in order to make a deal with Palestine he would have to at least make them believe he was somewhat neutral.

Trump said striking a peace deal with Israel and Palestine would be “maybe the toughest negotiation of all time.”

Cruz said Thursday it would be wrong to be neutral toward Palestine.

Islam and hatred

Trump and Rubio have exchange on Trump's comments last night that "Islam hates us."

Trump, asked if all Islam hates the United States:  “I mean a lot of them. There’s tremendous hatred.”

Rubio: A lot of people like Trump because he says what they wish they could say. But when you're president, you can't say anything you want. It has consequences around the world. 

Trump: Marco talks about consequences.  Well, we’ve had consequences, like airplanes flying into the World Trade Center. Large portions of Islam want to use harsh means.  Women are treated horribly.  There is tremendous hatred.”

Rubio responds that he's not interested in being politically correct, but being correct. Islam has a radicalization problem that will require the U.S. to work with Muslims to defeat it. 

Kasich says Islam doesn't hate America, but that it faces a serious problem in ISIS. Islam doesn’t hate us, but a radical sect perhaps does, he said. 

Asked about the U.S. waterboarding and other torture techniques, Trump says, “We have to obey the laws.  But we have to expand those laws.  Or we’re being a bunch of suckers.  They are laughing at us, believe me.”

Cruz:  “We have never targeted innocent civilians and we are not going to start now.” 

'Civil' debate

Trump and Cruz started to go after each other, but Trump pulls back: "I cannot believe how civil it's been up here." 

Trump needled Cruz, who fought back. "If you have a candidate who has been funding Democrats, and the liberal establishment, it is hard to imagine how this candidate is going to take on Washington,” Cruz said. 

Trump declined panelist Dana Bash's invitation to go on the attack.

But Cruz kept trying to get a rise out of  Trump: “We have got to get beyond rhetoric of  'China bad.’”

Social Security

Rubio is asked about Social Security changes and retirement ages. Rubio:  “I am against any changes to Social Security that are bad for my mother.”

He repeated some of the key points from his Social Security plan, while trying to appeal to younger workers who are facing older retirement age. 

Trump got a similar question. He took the opportunity to knock the Democrats' plan and "boring" debates. "I will do everything in my power not to touch Social Security, leave it the way it is.  Not increase the age.  Leave it as it is.  I want to make out county rich again so we can afford it.” 

He vowed to get rid of waste and fraud to meet the increasing need but was challenged in a follow-up question. His answer meandered all over the map -- literally. He wants to bring wealth back to the country. 

Rubio said the numbers don't add up, eliminating the fraud won't meet the need and won't save Social Security. It will result in a debt crisis, he said. 

Cruz urges "political courage" to change Social Security and raise the retirement age. 

Kasich advocates changing Social Security but not raising the retirement age. 

Education standards 

Trump, Kasich and Cruz differ on Common Core standards. Kasich advocates local control of curriculum. 

Trump: “I want local education, get together around a school and make education great,” while working in that former candidate Ben Carson will endorse him Friday, and will get him involved.

Education has "been taken over by the bureaucrats.”

Cruz repeated his calls for abolishing the federal Department of Education. 

Guest worker programs

Trump, Cruz, Rubio and Kasich address guest worker programs and illegal immigrants. 

Kasich:  “We have to finish the wall.”  He says he supports path to legalization, not citizenship. If the U.S. didn't have immigration, "I'd be running for president of Croatia."

Trump:“H-1B is something  I use and shouldn’t be allowed to use it.  But I’m a businessman …and it’s sitting there to be used.”

H-1B is a non-immigrant visa in the United States under the Immigration and Nationality Act, that  allows U.S. employers to temporarily employ foreign workers in specialty occupations.

Rubio focused his answer on recent controversies about Disney employees. 

Free trade deals 

Donald Trump gets second question about how he runs his businesses and whether he will run the country differently. 

"I'm the one who knows how to change it," he said. 

Marco Rubio was asked whether he would support free trade deals. He said the deal with Colombia has benefitted Florida and that he would support any that are good for the U.S. 

Ted Cruz said he wants to support American workers and bring jobs back to America. 

Ohio Gov. John Kasich spoke first and got the first question about trade and blue-collar workers.  “I want to transfer,power, money and influence to where you live," he said.

 Opening remarks

Florida Sen. Marco Rubio made his opening remarks second, followed by Texas Sen. Ted Cruz. Rubio repeated his campaign stump speech. "I believe this is most important election in a generation.  At stake is our identity as a nation and a people," he said. 

Cruz spoke of Florida's importance to his family, welcoming his father from Cuba. 

Donald Trump addressed Republican voters who have excited the whole world, and the party establishment, pointing to the “millions and millions” of new voters he says he's bringing in to the party.

"I think the Republican establishment should embrace what’s happening.” Trump said.

In a much more measured statement than his typical rhetoric, he said the GOP will be successful at winning the presidency in November. 

Getting started

The candidates are entering the debate hall -- Ohio Gov. John Kasich, Sen. Ted Cruz, Donald Trump and then Florida Sen. Marco Rubio.

The debate begins with a moment of silence for former first lady Nancy Reagan, whose funeral is Friday in California. 

Then the Frost singers from the University of Miami sang the national anthem. 

'We have something to fight for'

Republican National Committee Chairman Reince Priebus addresses the audience in the debate hall. 

"These candidates are competing to see who is going to be the nominee" the party will unite behind at the convention, he said.

"We have something to fight for," he said, adding that any of the GOP candidates would be better than Democratic candidates Hillary Clinton and Bernie Sanders. 

Debate start and finish 

CNN anchor Anderson Cooper is assuring viewers that the debate will start at 9 p.m. and finish at 11 p.m. "It will be two hours," Cooper said on air. You can watch it on CNN or livestream it at CNN.com. 

Outside the Bank United Center

Among about 150 demonstrators outside the Bank United Center on Ponce de Leon Boulevard was Jessica Leon, 23, who said she was a former receptionist at Trump National Doral resort, owned by candidate Donald Trump.  She was there to support her former boss.

 “I believe if he can take care of his employees, he can take care of the country,” Leon said.

Leon said she was not bothered by an atmosphere of violence and racism that some say hangs over his campaign. “I have never heard Trump say to punch someone in the face,” she said.  “That would not be the right way to do things.”

A few yards away Carlo Maiorano, 43, and Mauricio  Benitez, 42, both of Fort Lauderdale, were keeping a close eye on their young children, who were hawking $20 black T-shirts to passing motorists. “Hey Obama,” read the front.  “You’re fired” it said on the back.

They, too, backed Trump.  “I love that he’s self-funded.  He doesn’t need to be doing this,” said Maiorano, a former New Jersey resident.  “He is a little hot-headed at times, but we would feel safe with him in charge.”

Across the street, Nancy Meinhardt, 58, a former Marco Rubio backer, was waving an American flag and holding a Ted Cruz sign.  She said she felt betrayed by Rubio’s vote for immigration reform in the Senate.  “We need someone who stands up for the Constitution,” she said of Cruz.

Daniela Ferreria, 18, a west Miami-Dade high school student, yelled loudly for Rubio, expressing her confidence that the former West Miami commissioner could save his candidacy with a victory in Tuesday's Florida primary.  “I have been going door to door for him,” said Ferreria.  “He’s going to win.”

Also on hand outside the venue were those lobbying for a $15 minimum wage, Democratic candidate Sen. Bernie Sanders, union rights, and a reduction in fossil fuels.

 “I teach sustainable living,” said John Van Leer, 76, a professor at UM’s  Rosenstiel School of Marine and Atmospheric Science.  “Sea level rise is a major problem, and Bernie is the guy who is saying the right stuff.”

 Among signs spotted on the street: “Donald, size does matter.”

Carson to endorse Trump

Former presidential candidate Ben Carson's longtime friend and business manager, Armstrong Williams, says Carson's backing of Donald Trump “shows respect to the voters. Donald Trump has earned these voters.”

“We need to rally behind the nominee, and you don't want to do it too late,” he said. “This country's in trouble. The Republican Party needs to unify.”

Two people with knowledge of the endorsement told The Associated Press that Carson will announce his support for Trump at a news conference on Friday. They spoke anonymously because the news had not yet been made public.

After meeting with Trump, Carson believes that the Republican primary leader is “sincere.” “He can see his love of this country.”

And although Carson hasn't been pleased with some of Trump's discourse, Carson “can see that he is willing to change to do better.”

University of Miami scene

For a second straight night, the 2016 campaign circus descended on a South Florida college campus. This time, it was the University of Miami’s turn to play host to a presidential debate, with four Republican candidates taking center stage at the school’s basketball arena, the BankUnited Center.

“It’s a cool experience, and it probably won’t be happening again the rest of the time I’m here,” said Evan Gruchot, 19, a freshman studying finance. “I tried to get a ticket, but unfortunately I didn’t win the student lottery so I’ll be watching on TV.”

As protesters and sign-waving supporters of Donald Trump, Marco Rubio, Ted Cruz and John Kasich massed near the debate hall, there were fewer students than usual on the leafy Coral Gables campus because of Spring Break.

Gov. Scott talks jobs before debate 

Gov. Rick Scott jumped into the media scrum before the Republican presidential primary debate Thursday. He said the path to success for a candidate is “jobs, jobs and more jobs.’’

The governor repeated the words “jobs’’ throughout his answers for 10 minutes. “That’s how I won in 2010,’’ he said.

Scott, a Republican, said he won’t endorse a candidate and won’t reveal how he’ll vote.

“We have secret ballots in Florida,’’ he told a reporter who pressed him.

Scott said he hasn’t decided whether he’ll endorse after Florida’s presidential preference primary. But he said he knows the best candidate for the job is “the Republican one.’’

He said when he ran for governor, he was an “outsider’’ and “businessman’’ whose opponent had all the endorsements.

Asked whether he thought Sen. Marco Rubio should drop out if he loses in Florida, Scott said Rubio is “smart’’ and can make the decision himself.
