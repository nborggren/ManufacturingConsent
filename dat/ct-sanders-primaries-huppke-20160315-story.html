As losses mount, Sanders must accept an unpleasant truth

Rex Huppke

Bernie Sanders was in Phoenix on Tuesday evening asking a crowd of fiery supporters: “Are you ready for a political revolution?”

Their raucous response indicated a decidedly pro-revolution stance, but the same couldn’t be said of people who actually voted in states holding primaries Tuesday.

Sanders lost in Illinois. He lost in Florida. He lost in Ohio and North Carolina. Missouri hasn't been called yet, but it's not like he's running away with anything there. 

Maybe Sanders was in Phoenix to get as far away from those other states as possible.

His speech hit the notes that have now become familiar to anyone paying attention: a handful of billionaires are running our economy; change comes from the grass roots; corporations wield too much power.

Those aren’t bad notes to hit, but for all the passion Sanders engenders, his Tuesday night losses at the hands of Hillary Clinton make me wonder why he feels the need to go on hitting them.

Simply put, he’s not going to win the nomination. I say that as someone who views Clinton as a deeply flawed and troubling candidate. But it’s true. 

Clinton’s new round of victories was an avalanche that blocked Sanders’ already narrow path forward. Is it still technically possible for him to win? Yes, but the odds are slim and he would have to win many upcoming state primaries by, as the Washington Post put it, “improbably large margins.”

In his Phoenix speech, Sanders said: “We are doing something very radical in American politics: We’re telling the truth.”

Are you, Sen. Sanders? I appreciate the fortitude, but I’ve wondered for a while exactly how truthful you’re being.

You’re promising things that sound fantastic — many things I’d love to see come to pass — without detailing how those things could be paid for or how you’d garner support to implement them in the first place. I suspect the reason there are no details is because you know, as someone who has served in Congress for some time, that the chances of your grand plans becoming reality are somewhere between slim and none.

You’ve been promising people a revolution, but it’s one that will only work if it’s followed by a number of other revolutions: a take-over of Congress by like-minded lawmakers; a breaking of the partisan gridlock that has prevented the current president from accomplishing much of what he wanted; a sweeping change in the beliefs and desires of the public at large.

Your unrealistic revolution is predicated on the outcome of other equally unrealistic revolutions.

Don’t get me wrong, I would love to believe that some of what you’re promising is possible. But you saying, with great conviction, that you believe in what you’re promising isn’t enough to make it come true.

You said in Phoenix: “The truth is not always pleasant. It’s not always what we want to hear.”

That’s telling, because I don’t think you want to face the truth. And I know your supporters, many of whom have taken their devotion as license to harass and insult your detractors online, don’t want to face the truth.

But the time is coming to accept it, no matter how unpleasant. Clinton is going to be the Democratic nominee for president, and the only revolution that has a chance of moving forward is the internecine one happening on the Republican side.

And we can all see how well that’s going.

Revolutions are messy. And yes, they are necessary, but when America is confronting the increasingly real possibility of Donald Trump as commander-in-chief, it may not be the right time for a revolution.

It might be time to set aside the dreams you’ve harbored for years, dreams of a society that fits your view of how the world should work, and focus on the moment at hand.

Your opponent is far from ideal, but she has you beat.

The crowds cheering you on toward a political revolution have been loud but never loud enough.

Maybe it’s time to be honest with them. And with yourself.
