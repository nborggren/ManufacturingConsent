Hillary Clinton got one thing very right about Social Security -- but not everything

Despite its position at the center of our national debate over income inequality and the declining fortunes of the middle class, Social Security got relatively modest airtime in Tuesday night's Democratic presidential debate. The attention it did get, especially from front-runner Hillary Clinton, was instructive. 

Clinton was responding to a question from moderator Dana Bash of CNN about whether she endorses expanding the program, as her rivals Vermont Sen. Bernie Sanders and ex-Maryland Gov. Martin O'Malley advocate. In her answer, she focused on enhancing benefits for women:

"We have a lot of women on Social Security, particularly widowed and single women who didn't make a lot of money during their careers," she said. "They are impoverished, and they need more help from the Social Security system."

We have a lot of women on Social Security. ... They are impoverished, and they need more help from the Social Security system. — Hillary Clinton

On this little-understood point, she is absolutely right. Although Social Security is designed to be gender-neutral, women, and especially widows and single women, are the group it serves most poorly. The poverty rate among elderly widows is as much as four times that of elderly married women. That's true for several reasons.

One is the structure of Social Security benefits. Except for married couples in which each spouse earned roughly the same wages during their working lives, most couples upon retirement choose joint benefits, which are set at 150% of the higher-earning spouse's stipend. Upon the death of one spouse, the benefit drops to 100%. That's a cut of one-third, even though studies tend to show that a surviving spouse's financial needs tend to be about 80% of what they were as a couple.

The women's earnings also tend to be suppressed during their lives by time taken off for caregiving, either while raising children or caring for aging family members. As a result, although retirement benefits are based on the best-paid 35 years of one's working life, women on average spend only 27 years in the workforce. That's why women's advocacy groups have proposed a "caregiver's credit" as compensation — say by assigning caregiver years a value equivalent to half the median wage for full-time work.  

"Instead of having zeros, she should have something" for those years, Terry O'Neill, president of the National Organization for Women, told me in 2012.  

One other factor is the reduction of Social Security benefits claimed by state and local public employees because of the government pension offset, enacted in 1977, and the windfall elimination provision, enacted in 1983. Known as GPO/WEP for short, they were designed to keep employees whose jobs were not covered by Social Security from getting excessive benefits, either via their spouses' Social Security earnings or their own from private-sector jobs they held before or after their public employment.

These provisions affect teachers and other public employees in states like California that don't require all public workers to be in Social Security. Most are women, 72% of the active members of the California State Teachers Retirement System, for instance. 

Clinton didn't propose any specific remedies for this enduring problem, but her focus on it is encouraging. That said, she left many Social Security advocates feeling a tad uneasy by stating, "I'm going to look for ways to try to make sure [Social Security is] solvent into the future." That's a formula that could embrace both revenue increases from, say, raising or eliminating the cap on the payroll tax (it applies this year only to the first $118,500 of wages), or cutting benefits — "a double-edged sword," in the words of Eric Laursen, the author of "The People's Pension," a history of Social Security.

Sanders and O'Malley don't leave themselves that wiggle room. Both have come out explicitly for raising the tax cap and increasing benefits for all retirees as a way to make up for the erosion of retirement resources from such traditional sources as employer pensions and personal savings.
