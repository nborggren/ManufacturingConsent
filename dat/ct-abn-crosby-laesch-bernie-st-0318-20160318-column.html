School board member takes "leap of faith" – and some heat – for Bernie

Denise Crosby

I was actually surprised John Laesch answered his phone so early Wednesday morning.

He'd been putting in some awfully long days as a paid staffer in the Batavia office of Bernie Sanders campaign in these few weeks leading up to Tuesday's Illinois Primary.

And Laesch had gone to bed only hours earlier, after rushing to Kendall County to check on the shortage of paper ballots that eventually led to some voting sites staying open a bit longer.

Plus, I figured watching his candidate surge toward the end only to lose by less than two percentage points to Hillary Clinton had to be the kind of defeat that makes it difficult to get out of bed.

But Laesch, although he admitted to disappointment, was surprisingly upbeat. The race was a tight one, with his candidate picking up 67 delegates. And he had a full day planned, which included heading to the Aurora Election Commission to check on the numbers there; then driving downtown to Bernie's Chicago headquarters where he planned to give the national campaign an honest assessment of what he thought went wrong.

"I learned a lot of things," Laesch said. "The mechanics of a campaign do matter. And there is definitely room for improvement."

Some would say the same thing about Laesch's first-year term as a member of the East Aurora School Board.

His seat at the table has been noticeably vacant these last two meetings, when the district's dirty laundry has been aired publicly over the contentious teacher contract dispute and the ouster of Superintendent Michael Popp.

But Laesch insisted he had to "prioritize what is important," And that meant working practically 24/7 to help Sanders win the Democratic Primary here.

It was so vital, in fact, Laesch "took a leap of faith" and quit his job (more aptly, "took a layoff") as a union carpenter last month to put in up to 18-hour days in the Batavia office where, among other duties, he trained first-time volunteers.

Even in hindsight, the politically astute Laesch – who unsuccessfully challenged Dennis Hastert in the 2006 Congressional election; and lost in the 2008 special election Primary by a razor-thin margin to now Congressman Bill Foster – describes this experience as "the chance of a lifetime" to get a candidate into office who truly has the working class at heart.

Laesch insists his presence at these board meetings would not have made a difference in the way the votes went down on Superintendent Popp, as he's continually been a vote against the majority.

"I had to make choices," reiterated Laesch about his decision to skip board activity since signing on with Bernie.

School board President Annette Johnson said last week she "was disappointed" in Laesch's decision to forgo recent board duties. It's not so much that his seat has been empty, she added, but that he's done an inadequate job of keeping colleagues informed of his intentions, especially when it came to committee work.

"We have a lot of meetings," she said. "We have to rely on each other to communicate."

I also heard from maintenance workers worried about the buildings, who said they could not get in touch with Laesch, a member of the buildings and grounds committee. And they expressed concern that a candidate who had run on a platform of change and serving as a voice for the workers would skip so many meetings.

Laesch admits he may have missed calls because his voice mail would fill so quickly while working for Sanders. But he insists the board was well aware of his intentions to work on this Primary campaign in advance.

And even though his candidate did not win on Tuesday, Laesch has no regrets and is proud of the "team effort" it took for Kane and Kendall counties to pull in a majority of votes for Sanders.

Laesch says it's "unlikely" he'll be asked to stay on as an employee with the national campaign because he was too often questioning the tactics of the people above him.

While "it was nice" for these national workers "to parachute in," he said, "they don't know Illinois. They need to listen to local people about what to do."

Laesch says he still plans to do what he can for Sanders, including talking with the super delegates, those party leaders and elected officials who don't have to follow the will of the people and can change their minds at any time until the Democratic Convention vote is taken.

He also clicked off a trio of career options: sign on again as a union carpenter; work for his brother, who owns a small family remodeling business; or take a job offer from FEMA.

While he wants to help "straighten things out for Bernie" by continuing his services as a volunteer, Laesch promises, "My voice will return to the East Board."
