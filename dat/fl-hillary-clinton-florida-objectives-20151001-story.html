Hillary Clinton hopes day in South Florida produces money, grassroots excitement and Hispanic support

Hillary Clinton has three critical objectives when she brings her campaign to South Florida on Friday: raising money, energizing supporters in the region and courting Hispanic voters.

The candidate is spending the day in the heart of Clinton territory in a state in which polling doesn't show the same kind of danger signs that have cropped up in early primary and caucus states or national polls.

"I see her being strong here. I see her volunteer base here in particular is extremely strong," said Cynthia Busch, chairwoman of the Broward Democratic Party. "There's been a huge base of support for the Clintons for a long time."

Clinton's rally in Davie is the first such event she has held in South Florida since she became a candidate for the 2016 presidential campaign.

Clinton supporters Marty Kiar and Nan Rich set a high bar for the former secretary of state. They and Busch, who is neutral in the presidential contest, predicted the event at Broward College's main campus would provide a jolt of energy for grassroots activists. They expect a large, enthusiastic crowd — something that will look impressive on TV and in Internet videos.

"There are going to be a lot of folks there to support her," said Kiar, a Broward County commissioner. "The support she has is enormous."

Rich, a former Florida Senate Democratic leader, said the event for grassroots activists is important. "She is the most qualified person to be president of the United States and she knows the issues. She obviously has to work on showing that to the average person out there," said Rich, who worked with Clinton on children's advocacy issues decades ago.

"She needs to be out there more doing that, connecting more with larger audiences. It's so important for her to do these kinds of events, getting people together and energizing them. We need energy in the Democratic campaign because so much attention is being paid" to the Republican candidates, said Rich, currently a candidate for Broward County Commission.

Clinton's support is down sharply in national polling in recent months. The latest Real Clear Politics average of national polls shows Clinton with 40.8 percent, 26.8 percent for Bernie Sanders and 20 percent for Vice President Joe Biden. On Aug. 1, the numbers were 58 percent for Clinton, 18.2 percent for Sanders and 12 percent for Biden.

In Florida, Clinton hasn't slipped as much.

The average of four Florida polls released in September show Clinton with 52.1 percent, Sanders with 16.6 percent and Biden with 17.2 percent. During August, the Real Clear Politics Florida average was 62.3 percent Clinton, 15.3 percent Sanders and 11.5 percent Biden.

"I don't see enthusiasm lacking for Hillary Clinton," Kiar said.

Kiar, who's knocked on nearly 6,000 doors as he campaigns for Broward property appraiser, said Democratic voters constantly bring up the presidential contest.

"Whenever they talk about the race, it's not about any concerns they have with Hillary Clinton or people on the Democratic side, it's concerns they have with Donald Trump and what he said that day," Kiar said.

Another important aspect of Clinton's day in the region is money. She is holding fundraisers in North Palm Beach, Miami Beach, and at the home of Mitchell Berger, a Fort Lauderdale lawyer and prominent national Democratic fundraiser.

Clinton plans to use the Florida visit to launch Latinos for Hillary and sit for an interview with Telemundo, the Spanish-language TV network, according to her campaign. The message is that "she will defend and stand with Latinos— because when Latino families are strong, America is strong," a campaign aide said.

Clinton and her husband, former President Bill Clinton, have been courting South Florida supporters for years.

Earlier this year, she was in South Florida for a series of fundraising events and also appeared this summer before the National Urban League national conference in Fort Lauderdale. On the same day as the Urban League speech, she appeared at Florida International University for a speech on Cuba policy.

For their part, Florida Republicans are hoping to take advantage of Hillary Clinton's visit to South Florida on Friday by reminding people of things they don't like about her — and attempting to taint other Democrats by association.

Blaise Ingoglia, chairman of the Republican Party of Florida, plans to highlight Clinton's use of a private email server, rather than the government email system, while she was secretary of state. Ingoglia sees that as unethical and believes it may have jeopardized national security.

Ingoglia wants two of the Democratic Party's leading Florida candidates to suffer by association since they are supporters of Clinton and polling shows voters have concerns over her email practices and question her trustworthiness.

He said in a statement that on Friday "Hillary Clinton makes her way to South Florida to convince donors and voters that her negative headlines, FBI investigations, and unsuccessful record are not hurting her campaign."
