Clinton-Sanders debate in Miami with less than week to go before Florida and other big primaries

Hillary Clinton and Bernie Sanders started playing nice Wednesday night, but then got into a lengthy battle over who was the greatest champion of immigrants — before uniting briefly to condemn Donald Trump.

The Democratic presidential candidates were competing for the votes of Hispanic voters, one of their party's most important voting blocs, in their last debate before critical primaries in Florida and four other large states next week. "El Debate Demócrata" was co-sponsored and broadcast on the Spanish-language Univision network, and much of the two-hour faceoff dealt with immigration, Cuba and Puerto Rico.

After her narrow, but jolting, loss in the Michigan primary, Clinton sought to hit the reset button Wednesday night. The front runner can't afford a poor performance in next week's primaries in Florida, Illinois, Missouri, North Carolina and Ohio. Clinton is depending on strong support from minority voters.

Clinton said immigrants and the nation would be much better off if comprehensive immigration reform was passed when it was advocated nine years ago by then-U.S. Sen. Edward Kennedy, D-Mass., an icon for liberal Democrats. She rapped Sanders for voting against it. The Sanders campaign quickly blasted out an email labeling it a proposal from then-President George W. Bush, who is despised by Democrats.

Sanders said he has long been an advocate for immigrant workers. He said Kennedy allowed him, as a new senator from Vermont, to hold hearings into the conditions endured by tomato pickers in Immokalee "and the semi slavery that those workers live under."

Sanders criticized Clinton's opposition to New York giving drivers licenses to undocumented immigrants when she was that state's U.S. senator. Clinton said that 10 years ago Sanders supported indefinite detention for people facing deportation.

Under sharp questioning from debate moderators, both candidates promised not to deport children and promised not to deport immigrants who aren't in the country legally as long as they obey the law.

"My priorities are to deport violent criminals, terrorists and anyone who threatens our safety," Clinton said.

Both criticized President Barack Obama's administration for the record deportations he's presided over, but Clinton did it indirectly. "I do not have the same policy as the current administration does," she said. Sanders said he agrees with Obama on "many, many issues." But not on deportations. "He is wrong on this issues of deportation."

Clinton mocked Trump, the Republican presidential front runner who began his campaign by bashing Mexicans, vowing to end illegal immigration, deport people in the U.S. illegally, and build a wall on the southern U.S. border. She slipped into a slightly Trumpian voice to imitate his bragging about his plan to construct a tall wall on the U.S. southern border. "It's just a fantasy," she said.

Sanders criticized Trump for resorting to "racism and xenophobia and bigotry."

But early on, both ducked when asked directly if they considered Trump racist.

Clinton called his rhetoric demagogic and said he traffics in paranoia. "People can draw their own conclusions," she said, but "you don't make America great by getting rid of everything that made America great." Clinton said Trump's attitude toward immigrants, Mexicans and the order is "un-American. I think what he has promoted is not keeping with American values."

Sanders also avoided a direct answer. "The American people are never going to elect a president who insults Mexicans, who insults Muslims, who insults women, who insults African-Americans. Let's not forget that several years ago Trump was in the middle of the so-called birther movement, trying to de-legitimize the president of the United States of America," Sanders said. "Nobody has ever asked for my birth certificate. Maybe it has something to do with the color of my skin."

Following a lengthy immigration debate and less-policy oriented questions over Trump, Clinton's emails and what she may have said to the families of people killed in Benghazi, Libya, the debate once again shifted back to the issues, with the candidates differing in their stances on Cuba.

Both candidates welcomed Obama's thaw of relations with the dictatorial regime on the island, but Clinton took a hard and fast line against the Castro regime.

"Both Castros have to be considered authoritarian and dictatorial because they're not freely chosen," Clinton said.

But Sanders said that the island had made advances in health care and education under the regime, and that he didn't believe "it is the business of the United States government to be overthrowing small governments around the world."

On Puerto Rico, Clinton said Congress must give Puerto Rico the authority to restructure its debt. She cited closed schools, health care denied, families moving to Florida. "What we see in Puerto Rico now is a lot of suffering," she said.

Sanders blamed his frequent target for Puerto Rico's problems. "That little island is $73 billion in debt." Who's at fault? "Vulture capitalists."

At the start, Clinton sought to claim one of Sanders' top issues: the effect of the economy on the middle class, which he says has been suffering because of the policies pursued by big corporations and greedy Wall Street interests. "My focus is on more good paying jobs with rising incomes for families, and how we prevent corporations from taking jobs out of our country," Clinton said, vowing to force companies to pay for jobs the take out of the country.

"Jobs are the No. 1 issue," she said later.

She continued her line of criticism from their last debate, Sunday in Michigan, over Sanders' opposition to legislation that funded the bailout of the automobile industry at the beginning of the Great Recession. Sanders said she was cherry picking parts of legislation to make him look bad. The legislation in question was the Wall Street bailout.

Sanders repeated his regular complaints about Wall Street control of the economy. "Tell corporate America and Wall Street that they cannot have it all." He used one of his frequent lines from his native Brooklyn, calling it a "yuuuuge" issue.

Clinton pushed back when asked if she would drop out of the presidential race if she gets indicted over her use of a private email server, instead of government email, while she was secretary of state. "Oh for goodness, that is not going to happen. I'm not even answering that question," she said. The audience cheered.

She was pressed about why so many Americans say they distrust her. As she's said before, Clinton said she finds that "painful" and acknowledged shortcomings in her political abilities. "I am not a natural politician in case you haven't noticed, like my husband or President Obama," she said. "I have to do the best I can."
